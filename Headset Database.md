## **ðŸ§© Legend & Key Terms**

* **HAC:** Hearing Aid Compatible (Explicit support or physical fit).  
* **Telecoil (T-Coil):** Has an induction loop for hearing aids.  
* **DECT:** Digital Enhanced Cordless Telecommunications (1.9GHz). Better range/security than Bluetooth.  
* **ANC:** Active Noise Cancellation (for the wearer).  
* **ENC:** Environmental Noise Cancellation (for the microphone).  
* **Clamping Force:**  
  * *Low:* Good for glasses/sensitive heads (\<300g pressure).  
  * *High:* Good for seal/active movement, potential fatigue.  
* **Multipoint:** Ability to connect to multiple devices simultaneously.

You are absolutely right, and it is one of the biggest "traps" in buying enterprise gear versus consumer gear.

I did a quick check to see if this is unique to Sony, and it turns out this is a universal issue with flagship consumer ANC headphones. If you are adding a "Warnings/Gotchas" section to your database, this is worth noting:

Sony WH-1000XM5: Wired connection = Audio Only. (Microphones are disabled).

Bose QuietComfort Ultra: Wired connection = Audio Only. (Microphones are disabled).

Apple AirPods Max: Wired connection (via USB-C to 3.5mm) = Audio Only. (Microphones are disabled).

The Technical Reason (The "Why"): These consumer headsets use a standard TRS (Tip-Ring-Sleeve) jack for wired audio, which carries Left Audio, Right Audio, and Ground. They lack the fourth ring (TRRS) needed to carry the Microphone signal back to the PC. To get a wired mic on these, you have to buy a specific 3rd-party cable with an inline microphone (the little distinct bump on the wire), which completely bypasses the headset's expensive 8-mic array and sounds significantly worse.

In contrast, an enterprise headset like the Jabra Evolve2 or Poly Voyager handles wired audio via USB-C, which carries both high-fidelity audio and the full microphone data stream simultaneously.
